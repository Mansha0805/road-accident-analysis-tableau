# Key Insights — Road Accident Analysis (UK, 2019–2022)

Derived from analysis of 660,679 accident records. All figures reflect the full dataset unless a year filter is specified.

---

## 1. Overall Volume Trends

### Year-on-Year Accident Count

| Year | Accidents | YoY Change | Casualties | YoY Change |
|------|-----------|------------|------------|------------|
| 2019 | 182,115 | — | 247,780 | — |
| 2020 | 170,591 | −6.33% | 230,905 | −6.81% |
| 2021 | 163,554 | −4.12% | 222,146 | −3.79% |
| 2022 | 144,419 | −11.70% | 195,737 | −11.89% |

**Insight:** Accidents and casualties have declined consistently for 4 years. The steepest single-year drop was 2022, where accidents fell nearly 12%. The 2020 dip is partly explained by COVID-19 lockdowns reducing traffic volume; the continued improvement post-lockdown (2021–2022) suggests genuine safety gains.

---

### 2022 vs 2021 — Severity Breakdown

| Severity | 2022 | 2021 | YoY Change |
|----------|------|------|------------|
| Fatal | 2,855 | 3,882 | **−26.40%** |
| Serious | 27,045 | 32,319 | −16.30% |
| Slight | 165,837 | 185,784 | −10.74% |
| **Total** | **195,737** | **222,146** | **−11.89%** |

**Insight:** Fatal casualty reduction (−26.40%) significantly outpaced overall casualty reduction (−11.89%), which is the most meaningful safety metric. This indicates that while accident frequency is down, the severity of accidents that do occur is also improving.

## 3. Vehicle Type Analysis

### Fatal Casualties by Vehicle Type (2022)

| Vehicle Type | Fatal Casualties | YoY Change |
|---|---|---|
| Car | 2,350 | −23.03% |
| Goods 7.5t+ | 239 | −32.29% |
| Motorcycle 500cc+ | 176 | −46.99% |
| Bus/Coach | 50 | −54.95% |
| Motorcycle (small) | 33 | +50.00% |
| Agricultural vehicle | 7 | −12.50% |

**Insights:**
- **Cars dominate** at 82% of fatal casualties — proportional to their overwhelming share of total vehicle trips.
- **Motorcycles over 500cc** saw the largest improvement (−46.99%), though they remain disproportionately fatal relative to their road share.
- **Small motorcycles (≤125cc)** are the only vehicle category that *increased* in fatal casualties (+50%) — a counter-trend worth investigating, potentially linked to delivery rider growth post-COVID.
- **Heavy goods vehicles** (239 fatal casualties) represent a high-consequence category given the severity they inflict on other road users.

## 4. Weather Conditions Analysis

### Fatal Casualties by Weather (2022)

| Condition | Fatal Casualties | % of Fatal |
|---|---|---|
| Fine no high winds | 2,462 | 85.01% |
| Raining no high winds | 245 | 8.44% |
| Others | 117 | 4.03% |
| Raining + high winds | — | — |
| Snow/Fog | 73 | 2.52% |

**Key Insight — Counter-intuitive finding:** **85% of fatal casualties occurred in fine, calm weather** — not rain, fog, or ice. This is one of the most important findings in the dataset.

This does *not* mean good weather is more dangerous per trip. It reflects:
1. The vast majority of driving happens in fine weather (volume effect)
2. Drivers lower their guard and speed in good conditions
3. Fatigue and distraction (not weather) are the dominant fatal accident causes

**Implication:** Road safety campaigns that focus only on adverse weather miss the majority of the problem. Behavioural interventions (distraction, speed, fatigue) in normal conditions are more impactful.

## 5. Road Surface Analysis

### Fatal Casualties by Road Surface (2022)

| Surface | Fatal Casualties | % of Fatal |
|---|---|---|
| Dry | 1,959 | 67.60% |
| Wet or damp | 772 | 26.65% |
| Frost/Snow | 165 | 5.71% |
| Unknown | ~1 | 0.04% |

**Insight:** Consistent with the weather finding — **67.6% of fatal accidents happen on dry roads**. Wet roads (26.7%) account for a higher-than-proportional share of fatalities relative to their share of total driving conditions. Frost/Snow is dangerous per-mile but rare in absolute terms.

## 6. Road Type Analysis

### Fatal Casualties by Road Type (2022)

| Road Type | Fatal Casualties |
|---|---|
| Single carriageway | 2,113 |
| Dual carriageway | 677 |
| Roundabout | 35 |
| Slip road | 15 |
| One way street | 14 |

**Insight:** **Single carriageways account for 73% of fatal casualties** despite not being the highest-speed roads. Key risk factors on single carriageways:
- Head-on collision risk (no central barrier)
- Pedestrian and cyclist exposure
- Rural single carriageways with high speeds and infrequent emergency response

Dual carriageways (677 fatals) present risk from high speeds and lane-change incidents.

**Insight:** Accident volume largely tracks population and traffic density. Birmingham's dominance reflects both its population size and extensive road network. **Note:** High accident *volume* ≠ high fatality *rate*. Urban areas have more accidents but typically lower fatality rates due to lower speeds. Rural areas have fewer absolute accidents but a higher fatal-to-slight ratio.

## 8. Urban vs Rural Split

| Area | Accidents | % |
|------|-----------|---|
| Urban | 421,663 | 63.8% |
| Rural | 238,990 | 36.2% |

**Insight:** Urban areas generate 63.8% of accidents by volume. However, rural roads consistently show higher fatality rates per accident — due to higher speeds, longer emergency response times, and single-carriageway prevalence. Both areas require different intervention strategies.

**Insight:** The 5.7% of accidents occurring in **darkness with no street lighting** is the most dangerous subgroup — these locations represent under-investment in road lighting infrastructure. The combination of darkness and no lighting creates severely elevated fatality risk despite low accident volume.
